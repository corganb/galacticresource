Exoplanet habitability and stellar neighborhood statistics

Review of the exoplanet population statistics relevant to habitability - the eta-Earth occurrence rate from Kepler, the ongoing TESS and PLATO surveys, the M-dwarf habitable-zone-planet inventory, and the stellar-neighbourhood census within 10 parsecs.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Approximately 5,500 exoplanets have been confirmed as of 2026, with most discovered through transit photometry from the Kepler (2009-2018) and TESS (2018-) missions and the radial-velocity programmes at multiple ground-based observatories. The population statistics enable robust estimates of the frequency of Earth-size planets in habitable zones around different stellar types. The local stellar-neighbourhood census, refined through Gaia DR3 (2022), provides the catalog of nearest planet-hosting candidates for direct atmospheric characterisation. This report reviews the relevant statistics.

The Kepler eta-Earth measurement

Kepler's mission focused on Sun-like stars (G and K main-sequence) and produced the canonical measurement of eta-Earth - the occurrence rate of Earth-size planets in the habitable zone. The eta-Earth value depends on the precise definition of habitable zone and Earth-size, with current estimates ranging from 0.05 to 0.5 (5-50 percent of Sun-like stars host an Earth-size habitable-zone planet). The systematic uncertainty is dominated by Kepler's incomplete sensitivity to long-period planets; the inner-edge of the habitable zone is well-sampled, but the outer-edge requires extrapolation.

M-dwarf habitable-zone planets

M-dwarf stars (mass less than 0.6 solar masses) host a substantial fraction of all stars in the Galaxy and have habitable zones close to the star (corresponding to short orbital periods of weeks). The TRAPPIST-1 system (seven Earth-size planets, three in the habitable zone) and the Proxima Centauri b discovery have made M-dwarf habitability a high-priority research thread. The eta-Earth rate for M-dwarfs from TESS and ground-based surveys is approximately 0.16 per star, comparable to or slightly higher than the Sun-like star value. The total population of habitable-zone M-dwarf planets in the Galaxy is therefore very large (tens of billions).

M-dwarf habitability is complicated by stellar-activity effects: M-dwarfs are typically more flare-active than Sun-like stars, with extreme-ultraviolet and X-ray flux variability that may strip atmospheres or sterilise surfaces. The TRAPPIST-1 system, in particular, exhibits substantial flare activity. The flare-activity effect is mass-dependent (less active for cooler M-dwarfs), creating an interaction between intrinsic habitability conditions and stellar-environmental hazards.

The 10-parsec stellar census

Approximately 350 stars and substellar objects are known within 10 parsecs (32.6 light-years) of the Sun. Gaia DR3 (2022) provides the most-complete recent catalogue, with measured parallaxes and proper motions for nearly all members. The 10-pc population is dominated by M-dwarfs (approximately 80 percent of the count), with substantially fewer K-dwarfs, G-dwarfs (Sun-like, including Alpha Centauri A and B), and rare F-stars (Sirius A and Procyon A). The 10-pc census includes the nearest stars (Proxima Centauri at 4.24 ly, Alpha Centauri AB at 4.37 ly, Barnard's Star at 5.96 ly, Wolf 359 at 7.78 ly, Lalande 21185 at 8.31 ly).

Habitable-zone planet candidates near Sol

Multiple confirmed habitable-zone planets are within 10 parsecs of Sol:

Proxima Centauri b: Earth-mass planet in the habitable zone of the nearest star. Discovered through radial-velocity in 2016. The host star is a flare-active M5.5V dwarf.

Wolf 1061c: habitable-zone planet around a nearby M-dwarf at approximately 14 light-years.

Ross 128 b: Earth-mass habitable-zone planet around a quiet M4 dwarf at approximately 11 light-years.

YZ Ceti d: Earth-mass habitable-zone planet around a nearby M-dwarf at approximately 12 light-years.

Additional candidates exist around Sun-like stars at 10-20 parsec distances.

Direct-imaging targets

The next generation of large ground-based and space-based telescopes (the Habitable Worlds Observatory, the European Extremely Large Telescope) will pursue direct imaging and atmospheric characterisation of habitable-zone planets at 10-15 parsec distances. The target list is dominated by Sun-like stars where the habitable zone is sufficiently far from the host that direct imaging is achievable, and the planet is bright enough for spectroscopic characterisation. The Habitable Worlds Observatory, planned for 2030s launch, has a target list of approximately 100 nearby stars for direct imaging of any potential habitable-zone planet.

Outlook

TESS continues operation through the late 2020s, extending the transiting-planet catalogue particularly for the brightest nearby stars. The PLATO mission (ESA, scheduled 2026 launch) will provide ultra-high-precision photometry for Sun-like stars. The Habitable Worlds Observatory will be the first dedicated mission for direct atmospheric characterisation of Earth-like exoplanets. The combined observational programme is expected to identify the first specific candidates for biosignature investigation through the late 2030s and 2040s.
