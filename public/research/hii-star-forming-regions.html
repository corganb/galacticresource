HII regions and star-forming complexes in the Milky Way

Review of the Galactic HII region population, the WISE all-sky catalogue, the spiral-arm tracing methodology, and the relationship between HII region statistics and the Galactic star-formation rate.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

HII regions are interstellar clouds of ionised hydrogen surrounding hot, young, massive stars. They are the most directly visible signatures of recent star formation in spiral galaxies and have been the principal tracers of the Milky Way spiral structure since their early radio identification. The all-sky WISE infrared catalogue, combined with radio recombination line surveys, has produced a comprehensive census of Galactic HII regions through the past decade. This report reviews the HII region population and its connection to Galactic star formation.

Physical setting

HII regions are produced when ultraviolet photons from O- and B-type main-sequence stars (effective temperature greater than approximately 25,000 K) ionise the surrounding hydrogen gas. The radiation creates a region of ionised gas with characteristic temperature approximately 10,000 K, surrounded by a transition zone (the photo-dissociation region, PDR) and an outer molecular cloud. The HII region has a characteristic Stromgren radius set by the balance between ionising photon flux and recombination rate. Bright optical HII regions like the Orion Nebula are local examples; most Galactic HII regions are obscured by dust extinction and are detected primarily in radio and infrared.

WISE catalogue

The Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer (WISE) mission produced an all-sky 3.4-22 micron infrared survey from 2009-2011, with continued operation through the NEOWISE re-activation. The mid-infrared imagery resolves HII regions through the characteristic 12-22 micron warm-dust emission from PDRs and from the heated dust within the HII region. The Anderson et al. (2014) WISE HII region catalogue identified approximately 8,000 HII regions and HII-region candidates across the Galactic plane, more than tripling the previously-catalogued count.

Radio recombination lines

Confirmation of HII region candidates and measurement of their kinematic properties uses radio recombination line (RRL) observations, primarily at H-alpha radio frequencies (e.g. H92-alpha at approximately 8 GHz, H86-alpha at approximately 10 GHz). The radial velocity from the RRL Doppler shift, combined with the Galactic rotation curve, gives the kinematic distance to the HII region. The H II Region Discovery Survey (HRDS) at the Green Bank Telescope and Arecibo (until its 2020 collapse) produced spectroscopic confirmation of approximately 80 percent of the WISE HII region candidates and assigned distances.

Spiral-arm tracing

HII regions are concentrated in the spiral arms of the Milky Way, making them effective tracers of spiral structure. The spatial distribution of catalogued HII regions delineates four primary arms (Norma, Scutum-Centaurus, Sagittarius, Perseus) plus the local Orion spur. The spiral structure as traced by HII regions is consistent with the structure traced by maser sources, young open clusters, and Cepheid variables, providing a multi-tracer constraint on the Milky Way's morphology.

Galactic star-formation rate

The total ionising-photon production rate from the catalogued HII regions, combined with the typical lifetime of an HII region (approximately 5-10 million years, set by the lifetime of the ionising O-stars), gives an estimate of the present-day Galactic star-formation rate. Recent estimates from the WISE/HRDS catalogue, supplemented by Spitzer and Herschel infrared continuum measurements, give approximately 1.5-2 solar masses per year. The estimate is consistent with the rate inferred from other tracers (radio continuum, dense-gas mass functions, far-infrared luminosity) and identifies the Milky Way as a typical L-star spiral galaxy in the present-day star-formation main sequence.

Star-forming complexes

Many HII regions are clustered in star-forming complexes - associations of multiple HII regions, embedded young clusters, and surrounding molecular gas extending over tens of parsecs. The largest Galactic complexes include the Carina Nebula, NGC 6334, the Eta Carinae region, and the W43 starburst region. These complexes contain hundreds to thousands of solar masses of recently-formed stars and represent the high-mass tail of the present-day star-formation distribution. Galactic complexes are sometimes useful templates for understanding starburst galaxies elsewhere.

Outlook

The Square Kilometre Array (SKA) and the JVLA Sky Survey (VLASS, in progress) will provide order-of-magnitude better sensitivity for radio recombination line detection, enabling completeness extension to more distant and lower-luminosity HII regions. JWST infrared imaging is providing higher-resolution morphological characterisation of selected complexes. The combined data sets are expected to refine the Galactic spiral structure model and to improve the precision of Galactic star-formation rate measurements over the next decade.
