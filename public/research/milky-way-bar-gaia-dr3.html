Milky Way bar structure from Gaia Data Release 3

Review of the Gaia Data Release 3 stellar census and what the bar's confirmed half-length, pattern speed, and tilt mean for inner-Galaxy dynamics, dark-matter distribution, and pulsar timing.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy. The presence of a central stellar bar was suggested in the 1960s by HI gas-flow asymmetries and confirmed in the 1990s through near-infrared surface-brightness asymmetries from COBE/DIRBE. The bar's geometric parameters - half-length, viewing angle, pattern speed - were poorly constrained for decades because of dust extinction toward the Galactic centre and incomplete stellar samples. The European Space Agency's Gaia mission and its Data Release 3 (June 2022) have transformed this picture by providing parallaxes, proper motions, and radial velocities for tens of millions of stars within and behind the inner Galaxy.

Gaia Data Release 3 baseline

Gaia DR3 contains astrometric and photometric data for 1.46 billion sources, with five-parameter astrometric solutions (position, parallax, proper motion in two coordinates) for 1.32 billion. Radial velocities are available for 33 million stars from the Radial Velocity Spectrometer onboard. The mean astrometric precision at G = 17 magnitude is 0.1 milliarcsecond in parallax and 0.07 milliarcsecond per year in proper motion, sufficient to map the inner Galaxy at sub-kiloparsec spatial resolution and to detect rotational features in azimuthal-velocity maps with kilometre-per-second precision.

Bar half-length and orientation

Multiple Gaia DR3 analyses converge on a bar half-length of 4.5 to 5 kpc (Wegg, Gerhard, Portail 2015 and follow-ups using DR3 data), with the bar's long axis tilted approximately 25 to 30 degrees relative to the Sun-Galactic-centre line. The Sun therefore lies near the bar's leading edge in azimuth. This places the Sun at galactic radius approximately 8.2 kpc on the inner part of the Local Arm, with the bar's far end approximately 12 kpc from the Sun.

The radial-density profile of the bar is exponential rather than the box-shaped profile predicted by simple N-body models of bar formation. The exponential profile is typical of bars in external galaxies of similar mass and is consistent with secular evolution of an initially shorter bar over the Galaxy's history.

Pattern speed

The bar pattern speed is the rate at which the bar's pattern rotates about the Galactic centre, distinct from the orbital angular velocity of any individual star. Pattern speed determines the location of dynamical resonances - corotation, outer Lindblad resonance, inner 4:1 resonance - that organise stellar orbits in the inner Galaxy.

Direct pattern-speed measurements from Tremaine-Weinberg analysis applied to Gaia DR3 yield approximately 35 to 40 km/s/kpc. This places corotation at approximately 6 kpc, well inside the solar radius. Independent estimates from streaming-motion analysis at the Galactic-centre end of the bar give consistent values in the 35-45 km/s/kpc range.

Bar-disk coupling and the Galactic bar's effect on the Sun

The Sun's position relative to the bar's outer Lindblad resonance has consequences observable in the Solar neighbourhood velocity distribution. Several known kinematic features (the Hercules stream, the Hyades stream) are now understood as resonant features induced by the bar. The kinematic structure of the local disc within 1 kpc of the Sun therefore carries information about the bar's properties and history that complements the direct geometric measurements.

Dark-matter implications

The bar pattern speed combined with the gas-flow rotation curve constrains the inner-Galaxy mass distribution. The most precise modern fits favour a dark-matter halo that is somewhat sub-maximal in the bar region: stars and gas account for approximately 70 to 80 percent of the rotational support inside 5 kpc, with dark matter providing the remainder. This is consistent with hydrodynamical galaxy-formation simulations of Milky-Way-mass haloes but in mild tension with naive expectations from purely cosmological models.

Outlook

Gaia Data Release 4, scheduled for 2026, will extend the time baseline of astrometric data and substantially improve proper-motion precision. The Vera C. Rubin Observatory will add multi-epoch photometric and astrometric data on stars too faint for Gaia, particularly along the Galactic bar's far side. SDSS-V, MOONS at the VLT, and 4MOST will provide stellar spectroscopic surveys at scales sufficient for chemical-evolution mapping of the bar. Together these programmes will resolve the bar's age structure and chemical history at a level of detail that is currently unattainable.
