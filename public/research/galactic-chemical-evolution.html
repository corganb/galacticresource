Galactic chemical evolution from stellar spectra

Review of the Milky Way's chemical-abundance history as resolved by APOGEE, Gaia-ESO, and GALAH spectroscopic surveys, the radial and vertical chemical gradients, and the connection to the disc and halo formation history.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The chemical-abundance pattern of stars in the Milky Way preserves a record of the Galaxy's star-formation, supernova, and gas-mixing history. Modern multi-object spectrographs have produced surveys of millions of stars, with detailed elemental abundances for hundreds of thousands. The combined data set has revolutionised the picture of Milky Way chemical evolution and identified specific sub-populations corresponding to the disc, bulge, halo, and accreted-satellite components. This report reviews the principal surveys and the chemical-evolution picture they have produced.

Major spectroscopic surveys

Three large multi-object spectroscopic surveys have dominated the modern picture:

APOGEE (Apache Point Observatory Galactic Evolution Experiment) is the largest infrared spectroscopic survey, with approximately 1 million stars observed at H-band (1.5-1.7 micron) resolution. APOGEE penetrates dust extinction and samples the disc, bulge, and inner halo. The current data release (APOGEE-2 DR17, 2022) provides abundances for approximately 25 elements per star.

Gaia-ESO is the European-led ground-based optical survey associated with the Gaia mission. Approximately 100,000 stars were observed at higher spectral resolution than APOGEE, providing complementary high-precision measurements particularly in the disc.

GALAH (GALactic Archaeology with HERMES) is the Australian-led optical survey at the Anglo-Australian Telescope. Approximately 600,000 stars have been observed at high resolution, with abundances for approximately 30 elements per star.

Alpha-element abundances and the disc bimodality

Alpha-element abundances (O, Mg, Si, Ca relative to Fe) preserve the type-Ia-vs-core-collapse supernova balance. Type-II core-collapse supernovae from massive stars produce alpha-elements rapidly, on the timescale of stellar lifetimes (1-30 million years). Type-Ia supernovae from white-dwarf binaries produce iron preferentially, on a delayed timescale (1-10 billion years). The alpha/Fe ratio in stars therefore tracks the relative timing of star formation and chemical mixing.

The APOGEE disc data have resolved a striking bimodality in alpha/Fe at given Fe/H: a high-alpha disc population corresponds to the thick disc (older, formed during a rapid star-formation episode), and a low-alpha disc population corresponds to the thin disc (younger, formed at lower star-formation rate after type-Ia enrichment). The bimodality is dynamically as well as chemically distinct: thick-disc stars have greater scale heights and velocity dispersions than thin-disc stars.

Radial and vertical chemical gradients

The Galactic disc exhibits a radial metal-abundance gradient of approximately -0.06 dex per kpc (decreasing Fe/H with increasing galactocentric radius). The gradient is strongest in the thin disc and weaker in the thick disc, reflecting the different formation epochs and gas-mixing histories of the two components. Vertically (above and below the Galactic plane), the metal abundance also decreases with height, with thin-disc stars dominating near the plane and thick-disc and halo stars increasing in fraction at large heights.

Accreted populations

The Milky Way's stellar halo includes substantial populations accreted from disrupted satellite galaxies. The largest contributor identified to date is Gaia-Sausage-Enceladus (GSE), accreted approximately 8-10 billion years ago. GSE stars have a distinctive low-alpha chemical signature and a characteristic radial-orbit dynamics, both of which have been mapped by combining Gaia astrometry with APOGEE/Gaia-ESO spectroscopy. Smaller accreted populations include Sequoia, Kraken, and Heracles, each identified through chemical-and-dynamical clustering.

Globular-cluster connections

Globular clusters preserve chemical signatures of their formation environment. Chemical-tagging analyses match globular cluster abundances to specific halo-star populations, identifying the original parent system for many clusters. The technique has identified clusters that originated in GSE, Sequoia, the Sagittarius Dwarf, and other accreted satellites. The combined cluster-and-field-star analysis provides a coherent picture of the Milky Way's hierarchical assembly.

Outlook

The next-generation surveys SDSS-V (operational, 2020-) and WEAVE (operational, 2024-) will increase the spectroscopic data set by another order of magnitude, with deeper sensitivity and broader spectral coverage. The 4MOST survey (operational late 2020s) will provide the southern-hemisphere counterpart. The combined data set is expected to provide a near-complete chemical-and-dynamical census of the Galactic stellar population to 50 kpc, transforming the precision of Galactic chemical-evolution modelling.
