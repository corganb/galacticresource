The Milky Way rotation curve and dark-matter constraints

Review of the Milky Way rotation curve as resolved by Gaia astrometric data, the dark-matter halo profile required to explain the flat outer rotation, and the constraints on alternative-gravity hypotheses.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The Milky Way's rotation curve - the distribution of orbital velocity as a function of galactocentric radius - is one of the most direct observational signatures of dark matter. The curve remains flat or only mildly declining out to radii where the visible mass alone would predict a Keplerian falloff. This report reviews the modern rotation-curve measurement, the inferred dark-matter halo properties, and the constraints on alternative-gravity hypotheses such as MOND.

Modern rotation-curve measurements

The most precise modern Milky Way rotation curves come from Gaia DR3 astrometry combined with spectroscopic radial velocities. Eilers et al. (2019) used red giant tracers with full six-dimensional phase-space data to compute the rotation curve from 5 to 25 kpc galactocentric radius. The resulting curve is approximately flat at 230 km/s out to 15 kpc, then declines at approximately -1.7 km/s per kpc to the outer measurement boundary at 25 kpc. The decline is consistent with the expected fall-off in a finite-mass dark-matter halo.

Gaia DR3 follow-up analyses extend the rotation curve to approximately 30 kpc using K-giant tracers. Beyond 30 kpc the tracer density falls and statistical precision degrades. The total dynamical mass enclosed within 30 kpc is approximately 4 times 10^11 solar masses - a factor of approximately 10 larger than the visible mass within the same radius.

Dark-matter halo profile

The dark-matter halo profile inferred from the rotation curve is approximately consistent with the Navarro-Frenk-White (NFW) profile from cosmological N-body simulations, with virial mass M_200 = 1 to 1.5 times 10^12 solar masses, virial radius R_200 = 200 to 230 kpc, and concentration c = 12 to 18. These values place the Milky Way halo at the median of cosmologically-predicted halo properties for L-star spiral galaxies in Lambda-CDM cosmology.

Several modifications to the simple NFW profile are favoured by detailed analysis. A core-cusp transition - the inner-density profile flattening relative to the cuspy NFW prediction - is consistent with stellar-stream dynamics in the Milky Way and is naturally produced in Lambda-CDM simulations including baryonic feedback. The modified profile remains within the NFW family and does not require alternative dark-matter physics.

MOND and modified gravity constraints

Alternative explanations for the flat rotation curve invoke modified gravity (Modified Newtonian Dynamics, MOND, and its relativistic generalisations) rather than dark-matter halos. MOND introduces a low-acceleration regime in which gravitational acceleration falls off as 1/r rather than 1/r^2, eliminating the need for dark matter while reproducing the flat rotation curve.

The Milky Way rotation curve alone does not discriminate strongly between dark matter and MOND - both can fit the rotation data with appropriate parameter choices. The discrimination comes from secondary observations:

Stellar streams. The detailed shape and velocity dispersion of stellar streams (Sagittarius stream, GD-1, Pal 5) constrain the gravitational potential at multiple radii. The streams are inconsistent with the simplest MOND profiles in the inner Milky Way and require either additional ad hoc complexity in the modified-gravity formulation or dark-matter contribution.

Wide-binary kinematics. Recent Gaia analyses of wide-binary star systems at the MOND-relevant low-acceleration regime have produced mixed results, with some studies favouring Newtonian behaviour and others favouring MOND-like deviations. The observational signature is at the limit of current data quality.

Large-scale-structure cosmology. Lambda-CDM successfully predicts the cosmic-microwave-background power spectrum, large-scale-structure clustering, and gravitational-lensing maps using the same dark-matter physics that explains rotation curves. MOND requires additional ad hoc components to match cosmological observations and has not produced an equally compact theoretical framework.

Implications for the Milky Way's evolutionary history

The dark-matter halo mass and concentration constrain the Milky Way's accretion history. Stream-fitting analyses have identified approximately 10 to 15 distinct mergers and accretion events in the Milky Way's history, with the largest being the Gaia-Sausage-Enceladus event approximately 8-10 billion years ago that contributed approximately 10 percent of the current stellar halo. The pattern of mergers is consistent with the inside-out, hierarchical assembly predicted by Lambda-CDM cosmological simulations.

Outlook

Gaia DR4 (planned 2026) will extend the time baseline for proper-motion measurements and improve precision at the outer halo. The Vera C. Rubin Observatory will provide additional photometric and astrometric data on stars too faint for Gaia, particularly along distant streams. Stellar spectroscopic surveys (SDSS-V, MOONS at the VLT, 4MOST) will refine the kinematic and chemical structure of the halo at scales unattainable from Gaia alone.
